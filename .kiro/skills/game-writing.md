# Game Writing Skill

## Purpose
Ensure all narrative content in Witness Interactive is cinematic, emotionally
resonant, historically grounded, and educationally effective. Every word
serves both immersion and learning.

## Voice & Perspective Rules
- Always second person present tense: "You grip the railing" not "He gripped"
- Never head-hop — stay in the player's immediate sensory experience
- Never summarize what the player feels — describe what they sense and let
  them feel it
- Keep the player as agent — things happen TO and AROUND them, they decide
  what to DO

## Scene Length Standards
- Narrative text per scene: 80-150 words maximum
- Shorter is almost always better — tension lives in brevity
- If a scene exceeds 150 words, split it into two scenes
- Choice text: 4-8 words maximum — urgent, decisive, present tense
- Consequence text: 15-25 words — immediate, visceral, specific
- Never resolve tension in consequence text — always leave the next moment open

## Sensory Writing Requirements
Every scene must include at least 3 of these 5 senses:
- **Sound**: engines, explosions, shouting, silence, water, metal
- **Smell**: smoke, fuel, salt water, burning, gunpowder, blood
- **Touch/Physical**: heat, cold, vibration, pain, weight, texture
- **Sight**: light, shadow, color, movement, scale, distance
- **Taste**: smoke, salt, blood, bile, fear

## Scene Structure Template
```
OPENING LINE: Drop the player into the middle of the moment.
              No setup. No backstory. In medias res always.

SENSORY BUILD: 2-3 sentences of pure sensory immersion.
               What do they hear, smell, feel right now?

SITUATION: 1-2 sentences establishing the specific decision context.
           What is happening that requires a choice?

TENSION: 1 sentence raising the stakes — what happens if they don't act?

[CHOICES APPEAR]
```

## Tone by Role

### Japanese Naval Aviator
- Tone: Disciplined, duty-bound, conflicted
- Internal tension: Pride in mission vs. unease about attacking without warning
- Vocabulary: Military precision, honor, formation, altitude, target
- Avoid: Vilifying or glorifying — this role requires the most moral complexity
- Key emotional truth: These were professional soldiers following orders they
  believed served their nation, many with private doubts
- Sample line: "Below you, the harbor gleams silver in the morning sun.
  Beautiful. Peaceful. Exactly as the photographs showed."

### American Sailor (USS Arizona)
- Tone: Ordinary, confused, then terrified, then determined
- Internal tension: Disbelief transitioning to survival instinct
- Vocabulary: Shipboard life, rank, duty stations, below deck, topside
- Avoid: Jingoism or Hollywood heroism — focus on the human, not the symbol
- Key emotional truth: Most men had no idea what was happening for the
  first several minutes
- Sample line: "The sound doesn't make sense. Too big for an engine.
  Too close for thunder. The coffee cup slides off the table on its own."

### American Civilian
- Tone: Ordinary Sunday morning, then surreal, then fearful
- Internal tension: Disbelief, curiosity, then the weight of what this means
- Vocabulary: Neighborhood, family, radio, routine, ordinary life interrupted
- Avoid: Making the civilian feel passive — give them real moral choices
- Key emotional truth: Many civilians drove TOWARD Pearl Harbor out of
  curiosity before understanding what was happening
- Sample line: "Mrs. Tanaka from next door is hanging laundry.
  She looks up at the same moment you do. Neither of you speaks."

## Choice Writing Standards

### What Makes a Good Choice
- Both/all options must feel viable — no obvious right answer
- Choices should reflect real historical tensions, not invented drama
- Each choice must connect to the AP theme of the scene
- Choices should feel like decisions a real person in that moment would face

### Choice Templates by Type

**Survival choice:**
"Run below deck" vs. "Help the wounded man" vs. "Find your station"

**Moral choice:**
"Follow your orders" vs. "Trust your instinct" vs. "Wait for confirmation"

**Strategic choice:**
"Report what you saw" vs. "Stay at your post" vs. "Sound the alarm yourself"

### What to Avoid in Choices
- Choices that are obviously good vs. obviously evil
- Choices with consequences that feel random or unconnected to the decision
- Choices that break historical plausibility
- Choices where one option is clearly the "game" optimal play

## Consequence Writing Standards

### Immediate Consequence (shown right after choice)
- 15-25 words
- Present tense, second person
- Sensory and specific — not vague
- Does NOT resolve the tension — opens the next moment

**Good:** "The ladder is jammed. Smoke fills the corridor below.
          Three men push past you going up."

**Bad:** "You made the right choice. You helped the wounded man
         and felt good about it."

### Flag-Based Consequence (influences outcome screen)
- Set flags with descriptive names: helpedWounded, followedOrders,
  alertedCrew, abandonedPost
- Flags should be morally neutral in name — no "goodChoice" or "badChoice"
- Numeric counters for things like: crewSaved, decisionsFollowingOrders

## Outcome Screen Writing Standards
- 100-200 words total
- Must feel earned by the specific choices made — reference flag values
- Survival outcome: specific, personal, grounded in their actual decisions
- Death outcome: dignified, historically honest, not punishing
- Every outcome should end with a line connecting to the broader historical moment

**Sample survival ending (Arizona sailor):**
"You surface forty feet from the Arizona, lungs burning.
 The ship is gone — not sinking, gone, as if it was never there.
 Oil burns on the water around you. A hand reaches down from a whaleboat.
 You will spend the rest of December 7th pulling men from the harbor.
 You will never talk about this day for thirty years."

**Sample death ending (Arizona sailor):**
"The magazine goes at 8:10 AM. You are one of 1,177.
 The USS Arizona will remain on the harbor floor, a memorial,
 your name on the wall above the water.
 They will still be identifying remains eighty years later."

## Historical Ripple Writing Standards
- Each entry: 20-35 words
- Present tense for immersion, then shift to historical record tone
- Connect each entry explicitly to a consequence of the attack
- Build emotional momentum — start immediate, end with long-term legacy

## Forbidden Phrases
Never use these — they kill immersion instantly:
- "Suddenly" — show the suddenness, don't announce it
- "You feel" — show what causes the feeling
- "You think to yourself" — all thought is to oneself
- "Little did you know" — breaks second person present
- "History would later show" — use the Historical Ripple for this
- "You can't believe" — show the disbelief physically
- Any anachronistic language or modern slang
